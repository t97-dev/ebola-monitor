South Sudan: High Risk — No Confirmed Cases (May 21)
South Sudan shares a direct border with Ituri Province DRC — the epicentre. Significant cross-border movement, 11,000+ South Sudanese refugees currently in Ituri, no prior Ebola outbreak experience, and no Bundibugyo vaccine make this one of the most vulnerable at-risk countries. All WHO, Africa CDC, UNICEF, and partner preparedness protocols are activated. Juba is approximately 400km from the nearest affected health zones. The country is also simultaneously managing a severe cholera outbreak (102,000+ cases), active armed conflict, and 300,000+ displaced people — all of which would severely complicate any Ebola response.

Humanitarian actors & response in South Sudan
WHO
WHO South Sudan country office has activated border health protocols. Key actions: strengthening surveillance at Points of Entry (PoE) along the DRC border including Juba International Airport; deploying Rapid Response Teams trained for EVD; and providing IPC supplies and technical guidance. WHO has explicitly identified South Sudan as requiring a higher level of international support than Uganda due to absence of prior outbreak experience. Cross-border coordination active with WHO DRC and WHO Uganda. WHO notes PoE screening must be immediately reinforced to prevent cross-border transmission from Ituri.
UNICEF
UNICEF coordinating Ebola preparedness support for South Sudan: supply pipelines for PPE and hygiene materials to high-risk border states; community health workers mobilised for risk communication in DRC-border communities; coordinating for 11,000+ South Sudanese refugees currently in Ituri Province. UNICEF explicitly flags South Sudan requires higher international support than Uganda. Priority areas: community engagement, risk communication, trust-building in border communities.
IOM
IOM monitoring cross-border population movement between South Sudan and DRC, focusing on fluid displacement patterns in border states (Western Equatoria, Torit, Gbudue). IOM data: nearly 300,000 displaced in Jonglei, 1.35 million people have lost access to healthcare following destruction of 26 health facilities — severely undermining Ebola detection capacity. IOM supporting PoE screening assessments at Nimule and Gangura crossings.
OCHA
OCHA coordinating South Sudan humanitarian response environment. Ongoing cholera outbreak: 102,000+ cases, 1,662 deaths. Insecurity restricting access in Jonglei and Upper Nile. 30,000 displaced people in Nyatim denied access. OCHA flagging dangerous compound crisis — cholera + conflict + displacement + Ebola risk. Any imported Ebola case would arrive in a health system already overwhelmed.
Africa CDC
Africa CDC convened South Sudan health authorities in both May 15 and May 16 emergency coordination meetings. DG statement directly names South Sudan. Support focuses on: cross-border surveillance and alert management; laboratory diagnostic support for Bundibugyo testing capacity at national level; IPC capacity-building at border health posts.
IRC
IRC explicitly flagging South Sudan as a priority cross-border concern. Notes the same US funding cuts that weakened surveillance in Ituri may have analogous effects on health system resilience in South Sudan border areas. IRC assessing Ebola preparedness capacity in its South Sudan operations.
MSF
MSF has significant operational presence in South Sudan. While Ebola resources are concentrated in Ituri and Uganda, MSF has reinforced IPC protocols across all South Sudan health projects as precautionary measure. Teams on alert for suspected cases in Western Equatoria and Torit border areas.
SS Gov
South Sudan Ministry of Health has activated PHEOC and is working with WHO on border health protocols. Active measures: multi-sectoral Ebola taskforce at national and state levels; screening at Juba International Airport; isolation/holding units at Juba Teaching Hospital and UNMISS Level II facility; national EVD hotline reactivated (toll-free via MTN and Zain). High-risk border states: Yei River, Torit, Maridi, Gbudue, Tambura.
US / CDC
US Embassy Juba remains operational and is part of the regional interagency monitoring group. US State Dept Ebola Response Task Force covers South Sudan in its regional scope. US providing border screening assistance at PoE in at-risk countries including South Sudan.
Key gap
No vaccine, no treatment: Bundibugyo has no approved vaccine or therapeutic. WHO estimates a vaccine candidate ~2 months away at earliest. South Sudan cannot use ring vaccination — containment depends entirely on surveillance, IPC, and isolation. The compounding crisis (cholera, conflict, displacement) makes this extremely challenging.
⚠ Critical accountability issue: The IRC officially warned (May 19) that US government funding cuts from March 2025 reduced Ituri health programming from 5 to 2 coverage areas — directly weakening disease surveillance at what is now the outbreak epicentre. WHO DG Tedros said May 20 it is "too early to say" whether funding cuts contributed to delays. Reuters reports confirmation was slowed by lab missteps — initial failure to test for anything beyond the Zaire strain.

Outbreak originated in Mongbwalu, a gold mining town in Ituri Province, DRC. WHO now believes it started ~April 20 and spread for ~2 months before confirmation. First suspected death April 20; super-spreader event (funeral or health facility) believed to have amplified transmission. Spread to Bunia and Rwampara, then to Kampala (Uganda). Ituri borders South Sudan directly. As of May 21: 11 health zones, 3rd largest Ebola outbreak on record.
